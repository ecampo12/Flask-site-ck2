Before we Start

Crusader Kings II is a game that take place during the early and late middle ages. The starting date for the base game is 1066 (867 AD if you have The Old Gods DLC, or 769 AD if you have the Charlemagne DLC) and ends around 1444. As such the game uses some old vocabulary. This guide will be using that vocabulary so let’s go over it:

  • Holds/ Holdings: Refer to the land held by a character.
  • Vassal: The landed subject of a ruler. The player character can have vassals and can be the vassal of a Lord.
  • Lord: The ruler of vassals. The player character can be a Lord and have a Lord. Note: your Lord can also have a Lord.
  • Casus Belli: A Latin expression that literally means ‘a case of war’. It is the reason a character is going to war. (More on Casus Belli can be found here.)
  • Demesne: The amount of land a Lord holds.
  • Court: The people that live and work in your castle.

The Feudal system

Land in Crusader Kings II is organized using the feudal system. That means there is a hierarchy that goes as follows (starting from the bottom):

  • Bishoprics, cities, baronies: Are ruled by Bishops, Mayors, and Barons respectively. These are the smallest land holdings. A combination of either three will make up the sub holdings of a county. If the only holds any of the three listed holding, it is game over for them.
  • Counties: Counties are ruled by Counts/ Countess. Counts rule over Bishoprics, cities, and baronies and other counties. Count are the lowest title a player can hold.
  • Duchies: Duchies are ruled over by Dukes/ Duchess. Dukes rule over a group of counties.
  • Kingdoms: are ruled by Kings and Queens. They rule over Dukes and Counts.
  • Empire: are ruled by Emperors/ Empresses. They rule over Kings. It is the highest title a character can hold.

Knowing the different ruler titles is important because it tells you what will happen to your land upon your characters death. For example, say you are a king with two kingdom titles and you have two sons. When you die, and depending on your inheritance laws, your kingdom would be split between your sons. This not only divides the kingdom you worked hard to build but weakens both kingdoms and leaves them open to potential invasion by your neighbors. Now if you were an Emperor the inheritance of your kingdom titles would not split your empire (assuming you only hold one Emperor title).
